Assimilation of Doppler Radar Observations (Radial Velocities) with MM5 3DVAR System

 

            During the past year, NCAR, partnered with Korean Meteorological Administration (KMA) and Seoul National University (SNU), developed a capability to assimilate Doppler radar observations (radial velocities) with MM5 3DVAR system. One case study has been conducted, and MM5 3DVAR showed positive impact of assimilating Doppler radial velocities on rainfall forecasts and its potential for operational application. The case was selected by KMA. It is a frontal rainfall event in Korean Peninsula. The Jindo radar captured the most part of the rainfall process in South Korean.

            In the task of radial velocity assimilation, the following four efforts have been made and completed. 1) The MM5 3DVAR has been modified to include vertical velocity (w) increments to allow the 3DVAR analysis including vertical velocity. This is important when radar data are included in the analysis and for small-scale convective weather system. 2) The MM5 3DVAR has been modified to include background cloud water (qc) and rainwater (qr). Because the 3DVAR system is now in cycling run, pass of the qc and qr to the next cycle can alleviate the spin-up problem the forecast model has in the integration. 3) The observation operator for Doppler radial velocity has been developed and tested individually and within the 3DVAR system. 4) A series of experiments assimilating KMA Jindo radar data for the June 10, 2002 case have been carried out. The system is stable and robust in the cycling run with more frequently radar data. Preliminary results from June 10, 2002 case studies are very encouraging.

II. Inclusion of background qc and qr in MM5 3DVAR system

 

In MM5 3DVAR system, the background fields could be MM5 input analysis, or MM5 output forecasts. During the first year of development, the MM5 3DVAR is mainly targeted to MM5 input analysis. There are no cloud water and rainwater in the system. As KMA MM5 3DVAR has been set up for cycling run, inclusion of cloud water and rainwater mixing ratios qc and qr becomes important. When the previous cycle produces cloud water and rainwater, inclusion of background qc and qr in MM5 3DVAR system enables the passing of these variables to the next cycle. This can reduce the cloud water and rainwater spin-up time when model integrates from the analysis in the cycling run.

The MM5 3DVAR system is now modified and the background cloud water and rainwater mixing ratios qc and qr are included. We analyzed the fields before and after qc and qr

 pass-over, and verified that the system modification is right.

IV. Preliminary results from a case study

 

The case was selected by KMA. It was a rainfall event associated with a cold front in June 10, 2002, which moved southeastward with a cyclone. The KMA AWS network observed a maximum 3-hr rainfall of 54mm ending at 18 UTC 10 June 2002.

In order to perform numerical experiments, we set up the KMA 3-hr cycling system running on NCAR IBM SP2 computer. The script files and the execution environment are designed according to the KMA semi-operational run. The Jindo radar data for the June 10, 2002 case were collected and processed (quality control and preprocessing). A series of experiments have been carried out for this frontal rainfall case (Table 1). From all the experiments in Table 1, we can see that there are 3 basic experimental categories, KMA operational run (RDPS), current KMA MM5 3DAVR semi-operational runs, and the modified 3DVAR runs with radar radial velocity assimilation. All the numerical forecasts start from 12 UTC 10 June 2002. The model configuration is the KMA domain 2 with grid-spacing of 10 km. There are 33 layers in the vertical. The horizontal grid points are 160X178. The main model physics include Kain-Fritsch cumulus parameterization, Reisner-2 microphysics and MRF PBL parameterization. The model boundary conditions are nested down from KMA 30-km domain forecast.

The purpose of these designed experiments is to assess the impact of assimilating the Jindo radar data (radial velocity) on the rainfall forecasts. We will mainly focus on this target in the review presentation. In addition, Table 1 shows that some sensitivity experiments were conducted. We analyzed the sensitivities of the 3DVAR analysis and MM5 forecasts to 1) different cycling period in 3DVAR, 2) different error factors in radar radial velocity, 3) quality-controlled (QCed) radar data versus raw data, 4) different cycling frequency of radar data in 3DVAR and 5) terminal velocity extracted versus non-extracted radial velocity radar data. The sensitivity analyses are mainly shared with our KMA colleagues.

            The main findings are listed below:

            1). The radial velocity blending to 3DVAR analysis increases the lower level convergence in the vicinity of the cold front at 12 UTC 10 June 2002. The wind increments distribution clearly shows northwesterly wind increases behind the cold front at 850 hPa. The upward vertical velocity at 700 hPa over the rain-band is also increased.

            2). Generally speaking, the root-mean-squire errors (RMSEs) of the forecasted surface winds with the assimilation of radial velocities are reduced, compared with the experiments without assimilation of radial velocities, especially in the v component.

            3). Verified against the Jindo radar data (radial velocities), the RMSEs of the forecasts with radar radial velocity assimilation is reduced.

            4). Verified against AWS rainfall observations, the threat scores (TSs) of 3-hr rainfall in the radar data assimilation experiments are increased compared to no radar data assimilation experiments. The most impact is within the first 3 hours of rainfall forecast. However, we found KMA operational forecast performed exceptionally well for light rainfall forecast (threshold less than 5 mm). The 3DVAR experiments obtained a more clear rain band structure, especially for heavy rain band. The heavy rain TS scores in the 3DVAR experiments are higher than KMA operational run.

            5). Sensitivity studies show that the rainfall forecast is sensitive to the 3DVAR cycling. The more cycling in the 3DVAR is, the better the rainfall forecast is. The rainfall forecast is also sensitive to the error specification in the radar radial velocities and radar data quality. Assimilation of the quality-controlled radar data obtained better results than assimilation of raw data.

            6). We found spin-down (spin-up) problems in the 3DVAR cycling run. More frequently cycling is not necessarily improves the rainfall forecast at the current 3DVAR developing stage. Further studies are necessary after the whole system is developed.
